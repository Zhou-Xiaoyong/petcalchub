Frequently Asked Questions

Our calculator uses the latest veterinary aging models based on DNA methylation (epigenetic clock) research published by UC San Diego and other institutions. The formula accounts for the fact that dogs age rapidly in their first two years, then the rate varies dramatically by breed size. While it's the most accurate method available to the public, individual dogs may age differently based on genetics, diet, exercise, and healthcare.

The "1 dog year = 7 human years" rule assumes a linear aging curve, but dogs age non-linearly. They reach sexual maturity around 1 year (equivalent to about 15 human years), and large breeds age significantly faster after year 2. A 10-year-old Chihuahua is roughly 56 in human years, while a 10-year-old Great Dane is closer to 80. Using the "×7" rule would give both as 70 — which is inaccurate for both.

Dogs generally go through these life stages: Puppy (0-1 year) — rapid growth and development; Adolescent (1-2 years) — physical maturity, behavioral development; Adult (2-7 years for most breeds) — prime years; Senior (varies by breed: small breeds 10+, medium 8+, large 6+, giant 5+) — increased health monitoring needed; Geriatric (upper end of breed lifespan) — may need specialized care.

📋 Quick Tips

  • Small dogs enter "senior" status around 10-12 years
  • Large dogs are considered seniors at 6-8 years
  • Senior dogs benefit from bi-annual vet checkups
  • Adjust diet and exercise as your dog ages

🧬 The Science

This calculator is based on research from the Dog Aging Project and epigenetic clock studies. The formula accounts for the non-linear methylation changes in dog DNA that correlate with human aging markers.
