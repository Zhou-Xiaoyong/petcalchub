Frequently Asked Questions

Toxicity depends on the type of chocolate and your dog's weight. The danger comes from theobromine, which varies by chocolate type: white chocolate has negligible amounts (~0.25 mg/oz), milk chocolate has ~44 mg/oz, dark chocolate has ~210 mg/oz, and baking chocolate has ~450 mg/oz. Mild signs typically appear at 20 mg/kg of theobromine, severe toxicity at 40 mg/kg, and life-threatening seizures at 60 mg/kg. A small amount of baking chocolate can be far more dangerous than a large milk chocolate bar.

Contact your veterinarian or an animal poison control hotline immediately — ASPCA: (888) 426-4435 or Pet Poison Helpline: (855) 764-7661. Be ready to provide your dog's weight, the type of chocolate, the amount consumed, and when it happened. Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by a professional, as this can cause additional complications depending on the situation and timing.

Symptoms of chocolate poisoning usually appear within 6 to 12 hours of ingestion and may include vomiting, diarrhea, increased thirst, restlessness, excessive urination, and a racing heart rate. In more severe cases, dogs may experience muscle tremors, seizures, elevated body temperature, and cardiac arrhythmias. Without prompt veterinary treatment, severe poisoning can lead to coma or death. Early intervention significantly improves outcomes.
